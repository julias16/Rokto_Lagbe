What is blood donation?

A blood donation occurs when a person voluntarily has blood drawn and used for transfusions and/or made into biopharmaceutical medications by a process called fractionation (separation of whole-blood components). Donation may be of whole blood (WB), or of specific components directly (the latter called apheresis). Blood banks often participate in the collection process as well as the procedures that follow it.

Today in the developed world, most blood donors are unpaid volunteers who donate blood for a community supply. In some countries, supplies are limited and donors usually give blood when family or friends need a transfusion (directed donation). Many donors donate as an act of charity, but in countries that allow paid donation, some donors are paid, and sometimes there are other incentives like paid time off. Donors can also donate blood for their own future use (autologous donation). Donating is relatively safe, but some donors may bruise or feel faint afterward.

Potential donors are evaluated to ensure their blood is safe to use. The screening includes testing for diseases like HIV and viral hepatitis. Donors must also answer questions about their medical history and take a short physical exam to confirm it's safe for them to donate.

Donation frequency depends on the component donated and local laws. For example, in the United States:

  • Whole blood: every 8 weeks (56 days)
  • Plateletpheresis: every 7 days
  • Plasmapheresis: twice every 7 days

The amount of blood drawn and the methods vary. Blood may be collected manually or with automated equipment. Most blood components have a short shelf life, making it hard to maintain a constant supply. This has led to interest in autotransfusion, where a patient’s blood is collected during surgery and reused, or is self-donated in advance. Though “donation” usually means giving to others, in this case, it includes giving to one’s self.
