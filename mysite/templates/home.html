WHAT IS BLOOD?

Blood is a specialized body fluid. It has four main components: plasma, red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets. The blood that runs through the veins, arteries, and capillaries is known as whole blood—a mixture of about 55% plasma and 45% blood cells. About 7% to 8% of your total body weight is blood.

WHO CAN DONATE BLOOD?

Most people can give blood. You can give blood if you:

  • Are fit and healthy
  • Weight between 7 stone 12 lbs and 25 stone, or 50kg and 160kg
  • Are aged between 17 and 66 (or 70 if you have given blood before)
  • Are over 70 and have given blood in the last two years

SIGNIFICANCE OF DONATING BLOOD

Donating blood is significantly important because it's the only way to save lives in a way that can't be replicated. Blood is essential for various medical procedures and treatments, and there's no artificial substitute. It's crucial for managing blood loss in surgeries, trauma, and chronic illnesses like cancer, ensuring a consistent supply of blood products for patients. Donating blood also has potential health benefits for the donor, including a reduced risk of heart disease and cancer.

BLOOD TYPE COMPATIBILITY

A blood type is a classification of blood based on the presence or absence of antigens on the surface of red blood cells. Human blood is divided into one of four main blood types: A, B, AB, and O, and is further divided into Rh+ or Rh−.

WHEN TO AVOID

You should avoid giving blood if you have an active infection, are currently sick, have taken certain medications, or have certain medical conditions. If you have a cold, flu, sore throat, or any other infection, you should wait until you are fully recovered and symptom-free before donating. Additionally, if you have a fever, a temperature above 99.5°F, or have recently had a blood transfusion or organ transplant, you should also wait before donating.
